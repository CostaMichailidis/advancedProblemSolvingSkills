Problems Suck, but Challenges are Interesting.

From one problem, we can identify many potential challenges.

Did you notice the difference in phrasing?

Problems such as these...

  • Solar power is not efficient enough yet.
  • Gas is expensive.
  • I'm Fat!
  • I'm always so Angry.
  • The girl I like doesn't like me.

...and in general, are phrased in a matter-of-fact manner.

Challenges such as these...

  • How to accelerate the improvement of solar power technologies?
  • In what ways might I rely less on gasoline?
  • How to get fit?
  • How might I use my anger to do good?
  • What might be all the ways to win her over?

...and in general, are phrased in such a way that they invite solutions.

Breaking a problem down into challenges.

Listen to the owner of a problem talk about it, and extract potential challenges. Ask who, what, where, and when questions to help them provide detail.

Ask Why is this a problem? and What's stopping you from solving it? in order to create a Web of Abstraction that maps the breakdown of the problem.

Take any of the challenges and reframe them in order to pursue an innovative understanding of the problem.

Each challenge can inspire a completely unique problem solving adventure.

Three Types of Challenges:

Action

Some challenges simply need action to solve.

Planning

Other challenges require some planning to solve.

Imagination

The most complex challenges require imagination.

Logic will get you from A to B. Imagination will take you everywhere.
